Load & Price Time Series

Hub Price ($/MWh)iThe wholesale electricity price at the ISO's primary hub in dollars per megawatt-hour. This is the real-time or day-ahead locational marginal price (LMP) — what generators are paid and large buyers pay.

Price Analytics

Price Duration CurveiAll hours sorted from highest to lowest price. The X-axis shows what % of the time prices exceeded a given level. A steep curve = volatile market. A flat curve = stable prices. Negative prices appear at the far right.
Avg Price by Hour of DayiAverage price for each of the 24 hours. Shows the daily shape — typically a morning ramp (7–9am), midday dip, and evening peak (5–8pm). Solar-heavy ISOs like CAISO show a midday price dip (the "duck curve").
Monthly Avg Price ($/MWh)iMonthly min, average, and max prices. The shaded band shows price volatility. Wide bands indicate months with scarcity events or extreme weather. Useful for spotting seasonal price patterns.

⚡ How This Dashboard Works

This tool runs a 7-stage analytics pipeline across all 7 US electricity markets (ISOs/RTOs). Each stage transforms raw data into structured outputs that power every chart, KPI, and table you see. Select any ISO tab at the top to switch markets. Use the nav to explore each analytical layer.

📥 Stage 1 — Ingest

Pulls raw electricity load, hub price, weather, and interconnection queue data for each ISO. Supports live API sources (ERCOT, Open-Meteo weather) or sample CSV files. Validates data against schema contracts and logs a signed manifest with SHA-256 checksums for data lineage.

  • Load: hourly MW demand from ISO feeds
  • Price: hourly LMP at the primary trading hub
  • Weather: hourly temperature at a representative station
  • Queue: interconnection project list from ISO filings

🔄 Stage 2 — Transform

Cleans and joins the four raw datasets into a single hourly panel with consistent timestamps, units, and column names. Normalizes the queue data into a standardized project table with completion probabilities.

  • Joins load, price, and weather on timestamp
  • Fills minor gaps via forward-fill
  • Outputs: staged hourly panel CSV + normalized queue CSV

📊 Stage 3 — Forecast

Builds a long-range load forecast using two models: a naive 24-hour lag baseline and a weather-adjusted regression. Backtests both models on held-out data, selects the best, then projects load 10 years forward under Low, Base, and High growth scenarios.

  • Low: conservative growth (efficiency gains)
  • Base: trend-line continuation
  • High: accelerated electrification (EVs, data centers)

📌 Stage 4 — Queue Model

Applies completion probability weights to each project in the interconnection queue. Uses P50 (base) and P90 (conservative) probability scenarios to estimate how much new capacity will actually come online each year by technology type.

  • Historically 60–80% of queued MW never completes
  • P90 is always ≤ P50 (fewer projects complete)
  • Output: expected online MW by year and technology

⚡ Stage 5 — Markets

Computes wholesale market analytics from the hourly panel. Calculates capture prices for solar and wind (what a perfectly correlated generator would earn), identifies negative price hours, measures congestion via a proxy spread, and generates the market findings report.

  • Capture price = generation-weighted average price
  • Capture ratio = capture price ÷ avg hub price
  • Congestion proxy = price spread between on/off-peak hours

💵 Stage 6 — Finance

Runs a full project finance model for a representative utility-scale solar project. Computes 18 scenarios across combinations of contract type, price case, and capex case. Outputs NPV, IRR, DSCR, and LCOE for each scenario.

  • Merchant: revenues at spot price × discount
  • Contracted: fixed-price PPA with adder above capture price
  • Debt: annuity-style senior loan over the tenor

Key Terms Explained

Definitions for every metric shown in the dashboard

📈 Market Terms

  • LMP (Locational Marginal Price) — The wholesale price of electricity at a specific node, in $/MWh. Reflects the cost of the next MW of generation plus congestion and losses.
  • Capture Price — What a solar or wind plant actually earns, weighted by when it generates. Solar generates midday when prices are often lower, so its capture price is usually below the average hub price.
  • Capture Ratio — Capture price ÷ average hub price. A ratio below 1.0 means the technology earns less than the average — a sign of price cannibalization.
  • Price Duration Curve — All hourly prices sorted from highest to lowest. Shows what % of hours prices exceed any given level.
  • Negative Price Hours — Hours when the price went below $0. Generators pay to produce. Caused by too much must-run generation (wind, nuclear) relative to demand.
  • Congestion — Price differences caused by transmission line limits. High congestion = infrastructure bottlenecks preventing cheap power from reaching load centers.

💹 Finance Terms

  • NPV (Net Present Value) — Total project value in today's dollars after discounting future cash flows. Positive NPV = the project creates value above the required return. In MUSD (millions).
  • IRR (Internal Rate of Return) — The discount rate at which NPV = 0. Compare to the equity discount rate in assumptions — if IRR > hurdle rate, the project clears the return threshold.
  • DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) — EBITDA ÷ Annual Debt Service. Must stay above 1.0x (lender breakeven). Banks typically require ≥ 1.20–1.30x minimum.
  • LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy) — All-in cost per MWh over the project life, discounted to present value. The minimum price at which the project breaks even.
  • PPA (Power Purchase Agreement) — A long-term contracted price for electricity output. Reduces merchant price risk; the contracted scenario uses a fixed strike price.
  • Capex — Capital expenditure to build the project ($/kW installed). A major NPV driver — lower capex directly increases returns.

📌 Queue Terms

  • Interconnection Queue — The list of power plant projects that have applied to connect to the grid. The ISO must study each project's impact on the transmission system before approving interconnection.
  • COD (Commercial Operation Date) — The target date a project expects to start generating commercially.
  • P50 / P90 — Probability scenarios. P50 = 50% chance projects achieve at least this MW online (base case). P90 = 90% chance (conservative). P90 is always ≤ P50.
  • Queue Attrition — Most queued projects never get built. Historically 60–80% of MW in interconnection queues withdraw before completion due to cost, permitting, or financing issues.
  • Active / In Study / Submitted — Stages of interconnection. Submitted → In Study (ISO impact analysis) → Active (studying detailed costs) → Approved → Built.

🌎 The 7 US ISOs / RTOs

  • ERCOT — Texas. Isolated grid, market-only state (no FERC jurisdiction). Known for price volatility and rapid wind/solar growth.
  • CAISO — California. High solar penetration, frequent negative midday prices, aggressive clean energy mandates.
  • PJM — Mid-Atlantic + Midwest. Largest ISO by load. Competitive capacity market (RPM). Heavy on gas, nuclear, and growing offshore wind.
  • MISO — Midwest + South. Wind-dominant generation mix. Low average prices driven by cheap wind.
  • SPP — Great Plains. Highest wind penetration in the US. Frequent negative price events. Low average prices.
  • NYISO — New York. Urban load centers, premium pricing in NYC zone. Growing offshore wind pipeline.
  • ISO-NE — New England. Smallest ISO. Gas-dependent with high prices. Aggressive offshore wind buildout underway.

Data & Methodology Notes

📋 Data Sources

This dashboard uses stylized sample data based on realistic market parameters for each ISO. Load shapes, price levels, and seasonal patterns reflect real-world market characteristics but are not live feeds.

The pipeline is architected to connect to live data sources (ERCOT API, Open-Meteo weather archive, ISO queue filings) when configured with real API endpoints in the config files.

⚠ Model Limitations

  • Congestion is a proxy spread — not a full transmission power-flow model
  • Capture prices use stylized generation profiles, not actual plant dispatch
  • Finance model assumes a single utility-scale solar project — not a portfolio
  • Queue completion probabilities are rule-based estimates, not ML-calibrated per project
  • Load forecast uses linear regression — does not capture structural breaks
